But as the study’s authors point out, politicians also have less   direct —  and less detectable —  methods at their disposal. They   suggest, in particular, that a politician might be able to elicit   benefits from companies controlled by others with the presumption   (though not necessarily the explicit promise) that the favor will   be returned. It’s what the study calls "indirect lobbying," to   distinguish it from the more immediate and more readily   observable methods that companies and other interest groups   exploit to influence government, like traditional lobbying and   campaign finance. ’ ’
