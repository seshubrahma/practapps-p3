And this was true, too, in the 20th century Japan, notably, had its own ideas. If Japan had followed the Ricardian doctrine of comparative advantage, it would have focused on exporting rice and tuna.   Instead, by dint of hard work, ingenuity, and more than a little national strategizing, Japan grew itself into a great and prosperous industrial power. Its exports, we might note, were such    as automobiles and electronics, not mere crops and fish.
