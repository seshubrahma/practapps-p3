Had the framers of our Constitution intended the "public use" language to have such sweeping scope, they would have selected a more expansive phrase for inclusion in the Amendment’s text, such as "general welfare. " Instead, the   "public use" language was more narrowly construed, as it simply embodied the founders’ understanding that ownership of property is a natural and fundamental right. History demonstrates that secure property rights are an indispensable condition for the preservation of freedom, economic prosperity and social harmony.
