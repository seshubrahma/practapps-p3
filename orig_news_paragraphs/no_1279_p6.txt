To revisit the very first police consent decree is to examine a microcosm of the agreements’ potential, and their limitations. Many of the issues, including poor training, racial bias, union opposition and the high cost to carry out the decree, could have been plucked from last week’s headlines. Under its decree, Pittsburgh curtailed strip searches, began documenting traffic stops, gave officers "cultural diversity" training and tracked civilian complaints. But from the outset, the police union balked, warning of "more   shootings, more drugs" and a spike in crime.
