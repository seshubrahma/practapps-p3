He also brought in the blues musician Cedric Burnside to offer his rendition of "Love Her ’Til I Die," along with Karen Elson, the luminous British model turned Nashville singer, who took to a microphone with a tune from her new album, "Double Roses," as models of both sexes ambled through the crowd. Even without the abundant bourbon, Mr. Reid’s show would surely have had the loose, homey quality of a party you hope will keep going. In this case it did. When it ended, the subterranean space at the Beekman became the "Speakeasy," and those who didn’t have an early call the next morning stayed on, dancing to the music of a jazz band, Billy and the Rock Bottoms, and a D. J. set by Mr. Marshall.
