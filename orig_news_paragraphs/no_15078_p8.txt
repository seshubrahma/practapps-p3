The framers didn’t have cruise missiles, so they expressed no opinion in the Constitution on whether using them to strike targets in other countries a thousand miles away constituted "war."   But the lawmakers of the 18th century were definitely familiar with the concept of a "limited war," military campaigns of supposedly limited duration, but more than a    offensive. It’s hard to imagine that they would have considered any strike  —   missile or cannonball   —   to be not an act of war requiring congressional authorization.
